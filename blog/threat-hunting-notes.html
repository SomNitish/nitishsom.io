What is threat hunting?

Threat hunting is proactive — you're not waiting for an alert to fire. You're actively going into your environment looking for attackers who are already inside but haven't triggered anything yet.

The whole point is that traditional tools (antivirus, firewalls, IDS) are reactive. They only catch what they already know about. Threat hunting catches what they miss — the unknown, the zero-day, the attacker who's been sitting in your network for three months.

Key idea Reactive = wait for alert, then respond.
Proactive (threat hunting) = assume breach, go looking, find it before damage is done.

Who's on the team?

Threat Hunter
Plans and executes hunts, analyzes findings, writes reports
Incident Responder
Kicks in when something is confirmed — contains, eradicates, recovers
Malware Analyst
Reverse engineers malware found during hunts
Threat Intel Analyst
Feeds current TTPs and threat actor info into hypothesis creation

The data sources you actually hunt in

Before you write a single hypothesis, you need to know what data you have. Every hunt is only as good as the logs feeding it.

  • Network logs — flow data, DNS queries, proxy logs, firewall allow/deny events
  • Endpoint logs — process creation, file writes, registry changes, network connections from host
  • Authentication logs — logins, failed logins, privilege escalations, account changes in AD
  • Application logs — web server access, email server, FTP, VPN
  • EDR telemetry — SentinelOne, CrowdStrike — rich process ancestry, parent/child relationships
From experience In the lab environments I worked through, Wazuh was the SIEM pulling in firewall traffic events, Linux security events, audit events, VPN events, and FTP logs. Having all of these in one place is what makes correlation possible.

IoCs — Indicators of Compromise

An IoC is evidence that a system has been compromised. Think of them as the digital fingerprints attackers leave behind.

  • IP addresses — known C2 servers, malicious sources
  • Domains — malicious URLs, phishing domains, DGA-generated names
  • File hashes — MD5/SHA256 of known malware samples
  • Registry keys — persistence mechanisms added by malware
  • User agent strings — unusual or malformed HTTP user agents
  • Unusual processes — parent/child relationships that shouldn't exist

Where to look up IoCs

  • VirusTotal — hash/URL/IP lookups
  • OpenIOC — Mandiant's open IoC standard
  • STIX/TAXII — structured threat intel sharing format/protocol
  • Threat intel feeds — ISACs, commercial feeds (Recorded Future, etc.)
Important distinction IoC = what happened (past) — a file hash, a malicious IP.
IoA = what's happening now — a behaviour pattern indicating active attack.
Hunting for IoAs is harder but catches attackers earlier.

Hypotheses — the core of threat hunting

Every hunt starts with a hypothesis. A hypothesis is a specific, testable statement about how an attacker might be behaving in your environment right now.

Bad hypothesis: "Something bad might be happening."
Good hypothesis: "An employee with access to sensitive files may be exfiltrating data via FTP before their last day."

What makes a good hypothesis?

  • Specific threat scenario — insider threat, APT, lateral movement, data exfil
  • Expected indicators — what exactly you're looking for (large file copies at odd hours, logins from new geographies)
  • Data sources — which logs will contain the evidence
  • Testing method — what queries, filters, or correlations you'll run
  • Expected outcome — what confirmed vs. disproved looks like

Where do hypotheses come from?

  • MITRE ATT&CK — pick a tactic/technique and hunt for its indicators in your logs
  • Threat intel — a new campaign targeting your industry → build a hypothesis around their known TTPs
  • Anomalies in existing alerts — something in your SIEM looked weird but didn't fire a full alert
  • Historical incidents — past breaches in your org or similar orgs

The threat hunting lifecycle

1
Preparation & planning
Define scope — which systems, which network segments, what time window. Choose your tools (SIEM, EDR, network analysis). Set a clear objective so you don't go down infinite rabbit holes.
2
Data collection & integration
Pull together all relevant log sources. Make sure they're actually ingesting into your SIEM. Missing a log source means missing an entire attack vector.
3
Active hunting
Run your hypothesis against the data. Look for anomalies, unusual patterns, deviations from baseline. Use correlation — one log alone rarely tells the full story.
4
Findings & reporting
Document everything — confirmed threats, false positives, interesting anomalies that need more investigation. Reports go to management, IR team, and feed future hunts.
5
Remediation & optimisation
Fix what you found. Patch, isolate, revoke access. Then feed your findings back into your detection rules — a good hunt makes your SIEM smarter for next time.

Lab walkthroughs — 3 real scenarios

These three labs gave me the most practical understanding of how hypothesis-driven hunting actually works in a SIEM. All labs were run in Wazuh. I'll share the hypothesis, my approach, and the key findings — not the full answers (do the labs yourself).

Lab 01 Unauthorised access from DMZ server
"There may be attempts to access unauthorised servers and ports from a Linux server located in the DMZ environment."
01Start with firewall logs — look for newly added rules. A rule added mid-week for SSH access to a DMZ server is immediately suspicious.
02Correlate with authentication logs on the DMZ server. High volume of SSH failed attempts followed by a successful login = brute force → success.
03After successful access — check what was installed. Yum/package manager logs show exactly what the attacker installed.
04Once you know the tool (network scanner), look at outbound traffic to see what they scanned. The attacker used the DMZ box as a pivot point to scan internal networks.
05Follow the lateral movement — from DMZ → internal network → successful SSH to an internal host. Full attack chain confirmed.
Attack chain: External IP brute-forced DMZ server SSH → installed nmap → scanned 10.10.10.0/24 on port 22 → brute-forced 4 internal hosts → successfully pivoted to 10.10.10.24
Lab 02 Compromised VPN account
"User accounts with VPN access may have been compromised and unauthorised access attempts may be occurring."
01Find the account with the most SSL VPN failures — this is your target account. Volume of failures = likely brute force attempt.
02Check successful logins on that account — look for geographic anomalies. Login from Germany (normal) AND Mexico (suspicious) = credential compromise.
03Track the tunnel IP assigned after the suspicious login. This IP is now your pivot point for the rest of the hunt.
04From the tunnel IP, look at all outbound traffic. Port 22 dominating = SSH scanning/lateral movement.
05Map the full post-access activity. After SSH success → process discovery (T1057) → searching for specific services = targeted attack, not opportunistic.
Attack chain: Brute forced VPN account → logged in from Mexico → tunnel IP 10.34.1.13 → SSH'd to 10.10.10.11 → ran ps → searched for proftpd process (T1057 Process Discovery)
Lab 03 Anonymous FTP misconfiguration exploit
"Misconfigured FTP services may have left anonymous user accounts open, potentially allowing unauthorised file uploads and downloads."
01Filter FTP logs for anonymous login success events. Any anonymous login that succeeds is immediately a finding.
02Identify the source IP and geo-locate it. External IP from an unexpected country accessing your internal FTP is a strong indicator.
03Find the FTP server that allowed it — check its config file for when anonymous access was enabled.
04Go backwards in time — who modified the config file? When? How did they get access to do that?
05Trace the admin SSH session that modified the config — this connects Lab 2 and Lab 3. The tunnel IP from the compromised VPN is the same source that modified the FTP config.
Attack chain: VPN compromise (Lab 2) → SSH to internal server → modified /etc/proftpd/proftpd.conf as root → restarted proftpd → enabled anonymous FTP → external attacker from China (111.1.2.3) accessed FTP anonymously
The big lesson Labs 2 and 3 are connected. The tunnel IP from the compromised VPN account (10.34.1.13) is the same IP that SSH'd in and modified the FTP config. This is how real attacks work — one compromise enables the next. Threat hunting is about following the chain, not just finding a single anomaly.

MITRE ATT&CK — the threat hunter's map

MITRE ATT&CK is the framework that organises everything attackers do into Tactics (what they want to achieve) → Techniques (how they do it) → Procedures (specific implementation).

For threat hunting, the most useful thing ATT&CK gives you is a starting point for hypotheses. Pick any technique, ask "do I have the logs to detect this?", then hunt for it.

  • T1078 — Valid Accounts → hunt for impossible travel, logins at unusual hours
  • T1046 — Network Service Scanning → hunt for port scans from internal hosts
  • T1057 — Process Discovery → hunt for ps, tasklist commands run by non-admin accounts
  • T1021 — Remote Services → hunt for SSH/RDP sessions from unexpected sources

Key takeaways

  • Every hunt starts with a hypothesis. No hypothesis = no direction = wasted time in logs.
  • Correlation is everything. One log source alone rarely confirms an attack. It's when you connect firewall + auth + endpoint logs that the picture becomes clear.
  • Follow the chain. A single anomaly is interesting. Three connected anomalies across different systems is an attack.
  • False positives are useful. They refine your hypothesis and improve your next hunt.
  • MITRE ATT&CK is your starting point, not your answer. It tells you what to look for — your logs tell you if it's there.
  • Document everything, even disproved hypotheses. They teach you what normal looks like.
